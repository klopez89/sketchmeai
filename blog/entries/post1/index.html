While you can start with generating images using the base SDXL model, this post is going to go over how you can generate images of yourself! To do this you will need to train an AI model using pictures of yourself.

 

Training your First Model

From the Models page, click Train New Model to open the New Custom Model form:

Your first name is a solid choice for your model name, but it can be anything as long as you don't use spaces or emojis üòã. In a future update, you'll be able to train the model on an object or particular style. For this training, you'll train the base SDXL model on a person. We recommend uploading a minimum of 10 images (max: 20). Important: The quality of images you provide as training data is the biggest driving factor to the quality of the trained model in regards to resemblance.

 

‚úÖ High quality training images look like:

  • Varied angles and distance
  • Well lit
  • High resolution (ideally 1024x1024+) Pictures taken from a modern smartphone will do.
  • Only the one person in each picture
  • Clear face images mixed with a few that include your upper torso

 

üö´ Low quality training images look like:

  • Same exact angle and distance
  • Poorly lit
  • Low resolution (below 1024x1024)
  • Only the one person in each picture
  • Multiple people in the shot
  • Blurry faces. Sunglasses. Big puffy coat and winter headgear.

 

Once your uploaded images have processed, click 'Start Training'. Congratulations, you've kicked off your very own AI model training!

 

Model training will take about 30 minutes for 10 images (and about 3 mins per image for anything more). Once training is finished, you will receive an an email letting you know your model is ready. Click the provided email link to take you to the generation page with the newly trained model auto-selected.

Quick Overview of Generation Settings

We'll provide examples below on how varying each generation setting impacts your images, but here's a quick overview of them.

 

We'll provide examples below on how varying each generation setting impacts your images, but here's a quick overview of them:

 

Prompt: defines what should be included in the generation. Referencing the model name is required

Negative Prompt: defines what should be avoided in the generation

Seed: determines the amount of noise the generation starts with. Easy way to vary output

Guidance Scale: controls how closely the generation will adhere to the prompt text

Lora Scale: impacts the extent to which the training data (i.e. your resemblance) is pulled into the generation

Denoising Steps: adds detail and removes noise from generated images

Image to Image: allows you to use any image as a starting point for generation

Prompt Strength: only applicable for Image to Image, controls how much the generation pulls from your reference image vs the text prompt

 

We've set default values for each setting, but you may find different values work best based on your own model and prompts.

Exploring the effects of Guidance Scale

Guidance scale (also known as classifier free guidance or cfg) controls how closely the generation should adhere to the prompt. A higher value enforces greater fidelity to the prompt, potentially leading to more accurate but less varied results, while a lower value allows for more creative interpretations. Guidance scale can range from 1 to 20.

 

The default value on SketchMeAi is 13. So let's fire off generations w/ the following Guidance scale values to see how our latest image renders across the spectrum of values: 1,3,5,8,10,15,18,20.

Exploring the effects of Lora Scale

Lora scale adjusts the extent to which the training data influences the generated image, blending the base model's knowledge with that from the trained data. A higher value will align more with your training images; however, you may risk ‚Äúoverfitting‚Äù your likeness (resemblance and style may look awkward). A lower value will align more with your text prompt vs training images, at the risk of reduced resemblance. Lora scale can vary from 0 to 1.

Exploring the effects of Denoising Steps

Stable Diffusion works by starting with an image of random noise and refining the image over multiple denoising steps. Each denoising step reduces the noise a bit more, adding detail and coherence to the image. You may find a point where adding additional denoising steps stops improving the quality of an image, so let's explore some values to get a better sense of this: 1, 5, 10, 30, 40, 60, 80, 100

Increasing to 30 or 40 does not have much impact, and even more denoising steps cause the image to become fuzzy. Keep in mind that the number of denoising steps is directly related to the time it takes to generate an image, so increasing the denoising steps will also increase generation cost. I'm going to stick w/ the default value of 20 denoising steps. Next, let's experiment w/ image to image!

 

Exploring the effects of Image to Image

Image to image is a technique that provides the model with a starting base image to then generate from. It's a way to direct the AI to modify or build upon an existing image rather than creating one from scratch. You can use any image you want as a reference (pro-tip: save an image to google drive or similar, if you aren't using an image that doesn't already have a url).

 

We'll show you how to use one of your generated images as a starting point. Click the three dots on your favorite image and select 'Use Reference Image'. Doing so will automatically copy the image url to the Image to Image URL field.

Let's start by understanding the effect of prompt strength, which impacts how much the generation pulls from the prompt vs your reference image. Prompt strength can range from 0 to 1. Note: prompt strength is only applicable when a reference image url is provided for img to img.
